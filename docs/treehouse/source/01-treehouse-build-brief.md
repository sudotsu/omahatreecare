# Codex Build Brief: “The Treehouse” for Midwest Roots Tree Services

## Project Overview

Build a new editorial and educational section for Midwest Roots Tree Services, an Omaha, Nebraska tree-service company.

**Business name:** Midwest Roots Tree Services  
**Website:** https://www.omahatreecare.com/  
**Primary market:** Omaha, Nebraska, and surrounding communities  
**Section name:** The Treehouse  
**Recommended tagline:** Practical Omaha tree care, explained from the ground up.

The Treehouse should replace the feel of a generic company blog. It should function as a locally focused tree-care resource that combines homeowner education, real project experience, local species information, storm and hazard guidance, and decision-making tools.

The goal is to create a content section that feels more useful, credible, and visually distinctive than a typical tree-service blog.

---

## 1. Strategic Direction

### Core Positioning

The Treehouse should be positioned as:

> Real Omaha tree situations, explained clearly by the people doing the work.

The section should help homeowners understand:

- Whether a tree may be hazardous
- Whether trimming, treatment, monitoring, or removal may be appropriate
- What affects tree-service pricing
- What to expect during a tree-removal or pruning project
- How Omaha weather, tree species, access conditions, utilities, and property layouts affect the work
- How to compare tree-service estimates
- When a homeowner should call a professional
- When a utility, city department, or other authority may need to be involved

The section should not feel like a stream of generic marketing posts.

### Editorial Advantage

The content should emphasize information competitors cannot easily reproduce:

- Original Omaha jobsite photos
- Real project constraints
- Local species and recurring problems
- Backyard access conditions
- Power-line and utility considerations
- Before-and-after examples
- Explanations of why a particular work plan was chosen
- Anonymized project stories
- Practical links to Midwest Roots tools and services

The content should show experience rather than simply claim expertise.

### Main Content Categories

Create three primary categories.

#### 1. Homeowner Guides

Search-focused educational content covering:

- Tree-removal cost
- Hazard signs
- Storm damage
- Trimming versus removal
- Estimate comparison
- Stump grinding
- Utility conflicts
- Access and property constraints
- DIY versus professional work

#### 2. Treehouse Field Notes

Real or anonymized project stories covering:

- The property problem
- Tree species and condition
- Nearby targets or hazards
- Access limitations
- Options considered
- Why the final plan was selected
- How the work was completed
- What homeowners can learn from the project

#### 3. Omaha Tree Profiles

Local species and condition guides covering:

- Silver maple
- Cottonwood
- Ash
- Bradford pear
- Bur oak
- Red oak
- Hackberry
- Honeylocust
- Other locally common species

These pages may later connect to pest, disease, drought, structural, and storm-damage topics.

## 6. Design Direction

### Overall Feel

The Treehouse should feel:

- Local
- Practical
- Calm
- Experienced
- Approachable
- Outdoors-oriented
- Clean rather than rustic or gimmicky

Avoid making the section look like a children’s treehouse, camping site, or novelty lumber brand.

The “Treehouse” concept should appear through subtle editorial and structural details, not cartoon graphics.

### Visual Concept

The design can use themes such as:

- Canopy
- Branches
- Tree rings
- Field notebook
- Natural textures
- Arborist observations
- Ground-to-canopy progression

Possible subtle design treatments:

- Thin branch-like dividers
- Tree-ring line patterns
- Small leaf or growth-ring icons
- Editorial labels such as “From the Treehouse”
- Callout boxes titled “Treehouse Takeaway”
- Field Notes using a documentary-style visual treatment

Do not overuse wood textures, dark brown backgrounds, or decorative leaves.

### Color Use

Use the existing Midwest Roots brand colors.

Codex should inspect the current site and extend the present design system rather than introducing an unrelated palette.

Suggested use:

- Dark brand green or charcoal for headings
- Warm off-white or pale natural background for content areas
- Muted green for tags and tool callouts
- A limited accent color for CTAs
- High-contrast body text

All color combinations must meet accessibility standards.

### Typography

Preserve the current website’s type system when possible.

For long-form content:

- Use a readable body font
- Set comfortable line height
- Keep the main reading column approximately 680–760 pixels wide
- Avoid overly small body copy
- Use clear heading hierarchy
- Make tables horizontally scrollable on small screens

The Treehouse should feel editorial, but it must remain consistent with the rest of the site.

### Photography

Prioritize:

- Original Midwest Roots jobsite images
- Full-tree context
- Tree defects or concerns
- Access conditions
- Equipment setup
- Work in progress
- Final cleanup
- Omaha residential settings

Avoid:

- Generic stock arborists
- Repeated chainsaw closeups
- Dramatic danger imagery
- Images that show unsafe practices
- Customer addresses or identifying details without permission

## 9. SEO Requirements

Every article page should support:

- Unique SEO title
- Unique meta description
- Canonical URL
- Open Graph title
- Open Graph description
- Open Graph image
- Twitter or social-card metadata
- Article publication date
- Article modification date
- Author information
- Crawlable internal links
- Descriptive image alt text
- XML sitemap inclusion
- Breadcrumbs
- Structured data

### Structured Data

Use `BlogPosting` or `Article` schema for Treehouse articles.

Include only verified values:

- Headline
- Description
- Canonical URL
- Featured image
- Author
- Publisher
- Date published
- Date modified

Use `BreadcrumbList` schema on article pages.

Use `FAQPage` schema only if:

- The FAQ content is visible on the page
- The current implementation and search guidance justify it
- It is not added solely to chase enhanced search results

Do not invent credentials, ratings, reviews, addresses, or service claims.

## 14. Accessibility Requirements

Meet WCAG-oriented best practices.

Include:

- Semantic HTML
- One H1 per page
- Logical heading order
- Keyboard-accessible navigation
- Visible focus states
- Descriptive alt text
- Sufficient color contrast
- Accessible accordion behavior
- Accessible labels for filters or search
- Reduced-motion support
- No important text embedded only in images

---

## 15. Performance Requirements

The Treehouse should not make the site feel heavy.

Prioritize:

- Responsive image formats
- Lazy loading below-the-fold images
- Correct image dimensions
- Minimal client-side JavaScript
- Server-rendered or statically generated article content when supported
- Limited font loading
- Avoidance of large animation libraries
- Good Core Web Vitals
- Stable layout during image loading

Do not add a large third-party blog framework if the existing stack can support the section directly.

## 19. Tone and Editorial Style

The copy should sound:

- Clear
- Calm
- Helpful
- Local
- Experienced
- Honest
- Practical

Avoid:

- Fear-based selling
- “Act now before it is too late” language
- Unsupported superlatives
- Claims that every damaged tree needs removal
- Artificial keyword repetition
- Generic AI-style introductions
- Excessive exclamation points
- Overly technical arboriculture language without explanation

Use plain language and explain technical terms when needed.

## 20. Important Content Rules

Do not publish:

- Invented Omaha pricing data
- Fake customer stories
- Fake reviews
- Unverified certifications
- Unsupported claims about tree risk
- Exact permit claims without official verification
- Statements implying that online screening replaces an on-site assessment
- Statements that Midwest Roots performs utility line-clearance work unless verified
- Customer addresses without permission

For Field Notes:

- Use real jobs
- Anonymize homeowner details
- Obtain permission for identifiable property photos
- Explain the decision process
- Avoid describing unsafe operational details in a way that encourages DIY imitation

## 21. Acceptance Criteria

The MVP is complete when:

- `/treehouse` is live and responsive
- The Treehouse visually matches the main Midwest Roots website
- The page clearly explains the three content categories
- The first article is fully published
- The article supports a table of contents
- Article metadata is complete
- Article structured data is valid
- Breadcrumbs work
- Internal links use real site routes
- The cost-planning tool is linked prominently
- Related service and contact links are present
- Mobile layout is readable
- Images are responsive and optimized
- Accessibility basics are met
- Empty category pages are not indexed
- There are no invented claims, credentials, prices, or reviews
- The system is ready for additional articles without creating a new page layout each time

The finished result should be judged by one question:

> Can Midwest Roots publish a genuinely useful new Omaha tree-care article afterward by adding structured content and images, without rebuilding the page or asking a developer to invent another layout?

When that answer is yes—and the first article is fully integrated into the existing tools, services, SEO system, and estimate journey—the Treehouse MVP is done.

2. Identify the current framework, routing approach, content system, design tokens, reusable components, SEO utilities, and deployment conventions.
3. Reuse the existing header, footer, buttons, forms, spacing, colors, and typography.
